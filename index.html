Property Damage Liability

Objective: Help the client understand what Property Damage Liability is, why state minimums are risky, and how increased coverage better protects them.

Agent: So, (Name), I’m going to pull up your current coverages so I can see what you have. I see you’re currently with (Insurance Company). How’s your overall experience been with them—rates, customer service, support?

Agent: While I’m pulling that up, I always like to ask this question: Let’s say you’re driving and you get into an accident where you’re at fault and there’s a large damage bill—how much would you expect your insurance company to cover?

Customer: All of it.

Agent: Right—that’s exactly what insurance is for. So now let’s look at what you actually have with GEICO.

Agent: You currently have 30/60 for Bodily Injury Liability—that’s how much your insurance would pay for the other driver’s medical bills if you’re at fault. We’ll match that here.

Agent: You also have Uninsured Motorist coverage, which protects you if the person who hits you doesn’t have insurance, not enough insurance, or if it’s a hit-and-run. It matches your Bodily Injury limits: 30/60.

Agent: Now here’s the part that really concerns me: Property Damage Liability. This covers what your insurance pays for someone else’s car or property if you’re at fault.

Agent: Right now, you're set at $15,000—the state minimum. That means anything above that comes out of your pocket.

Agent: For example, are you familiar with the Ford F-150?

Customer: Yes.

Agent: Know about how much they cost?

Customer: Around $60,000.

Agent: Exactly. So if you total an F-150, GEICO gives you $15,000. How much are you paying out of pocket?

Customer: $45,000.

Agent: And how does that sound?

Customer: Horrible.

Agent: Right—that’s why we recommend $300,000 in Property Damage Liability to make sure you’re protected. Make sense?
